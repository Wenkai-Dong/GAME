GAME: Gated Attention-based Map Encoding for Humanoid Locomotion over Discrete Terrain

Abstract

Although deep reinforcement learning has significantly advanced the perceptual-motor control of humanoid robots, enabling them to navigate various complex terrains through terrain perception, the success rate of existing strategies remains low on narrow terrains with only a single feasible path—such as narrow beams or single-file balance beams. In such scenarios, the feasible landing areas are unique and narrow, leaving virtually no margin for error, any deviation in foot placement will result in a fall, making such terrains one of the most challenging scenarios in perceptual-motor control. Attention-based map encoding, which uses body perception as a query to dynamically extract footstep-relevant local terrain from the robot’s center elevation map, has achieved significant progress on general sparse terrain. However, on the aforementioned narrow terrains, the terrain representation obtained through global weighting incorporates a large amount of irrelevant surrounding signals, making it difficult to sharpen the unique feasible footstep area. To address this challenge, this paper proposes GAME (Gated Attention-based Map Encoding), an end-to-end humanoid perceptual-motor control framework designed for narrow and extreme terrains. It introduces two distinct mechanisms into query-driven attention: channel-wise gating continuously modulates the terrain representation at the attention output based on data dependencies, amplifying feature channels carrying foot placement signals while suppressing irrelevant interference channels, enabling the output to focus more precisely on critical foot placement regions; channel-wise attention pooling aggregates the entire terrain into a global context vector and concatenates it with the query, supplementing the query with global terrain information so that the policy can localize foot placement points within a more comprehensive terrain view. At Isaac Lab, using the Unitree G1 (29 degrees of freedom) as our platform, we compared GAME with AME under controlled conditions—with the same MDP, terrain, and network capacity, differing only in the terrain encoder. The experiments demonstrate that, without compromising other terrain performance metrics, GAME significantly improves passage success rates on narrow and extreme terrains, validating the effectiveness of the proposed mechanism for this long-standing challenge.
